First-timers win big at Golden Globes

From "Glee's" Chris Colfer to "Boardwalk Empire," the first flood of awards went to first-time winners at the podium.

Chris Colfer picked up the award for supporting actor on a TV series for "Glee." (Mario Anzuoni / Reuters)

It wasn't the same old same-old at the 68th Golden Globes Awards on Sunday night.

Though some award show winners read like a copy of the year before, the first flood of awards all went to first-timers to the podium.

HBO's freshman 1920s gangster series, "Boardwalk Empire," laid waste to the competition, winning drama series as well as a trophy for Steve Buscemi for lead actor and beating such faves as "Mad Men" and "Dexter" and Jon Hamm and Michael C. Hall.

A visibly moved and emotional Chris Colfer won for supporting actor on TV for his role as an openly gay high school student on the Fox musical comedy "Glee." Hoisting the award, he said he was dedicating it to all the kids who watch the show and to those who are bullied. He also said the honor delivers a message to anyone who says kids can't be who they want to be: "Screw that!"

Other first-time winners included Christian Bale for supporting actor in a movie for his role as a drug-addicted former boxer in "The Fighter" and Katey Sagal, who won her first-ever Golden Globe for best actress in a TV drama for "Sons of Anarchy." The honor for best TV miniseries or movie went to the French miniseries, "Carlos," which beat out such HBO movies and miniseries as "The Pacific," "Temple Grandin" and "You Don't Know Jack."

Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross won for their original score for "The Social Network," and Diane Warren won her first Golden Globe for "You Haven't Seen the Last of Me" from "Burlesque."

Host Ricky Gervais got the show underway by insulting everybody.

"I warned them," Gervais cracked after an opening monologue that made fun of "The Tourist," Cher, the airbrushing that went into the movie poster for "Sex and the City 2," referred to Hugh Hefner as "the walking dead," brought up Mel Gibson's allegedly anti-Semitic leanings and, for good measure, questioned the sexual orientation of some of Hollywood's high-profile scientologists. He even alluded to allegations that the evening's host -- the Hollywood Foreign Press Assn. -- had taken bribes.

Though the audience seemed uncomfortable with Gervais -- always a risky choice for host -- several in the audience at the Beverly Hilton Hotel were laughing so hard they were wiping tears of laughter from their eyes, including actor Alec Baldwin.

One thing is for certain: Joel and Ethan Coen's remake of "True Grit" won't take home any honors because the HFPA snubbed the box office hit and awards-season favorite.

But as Gervais noted, the HFPA did embrace two critically lambasted films that failed to set the box office on fire. "The Tourist" is nominated for motion picture in the comedy or musical category even though it is more of a romantic thriller. And its stars, Johnny Depp and Angelina Jolie, are also nominated. The campy musical "Burlesque" is also nominated for comedy or musical, with three of its songs nominated for best of the year.

The rest of the dramatic feature film nods are in step with other critics' awards and guild nominations. "The King's Speech" leads with seven Golden Globe nominations, and "The Fighter" and "The Social Network," which has dominated this awards season, follow close behind with six nods apiece. Other nominees for the best motion picture in the drama category are "Black Swan" and "Inception." Meanwhile, "Alice in Wonderland," "The Kids Are All Right" and "Red" round out the nominees for motion picture in the comedy or musical category.

For lead actor in a drama, the front-runners are Jesse Eisenberg for "The Social Network" and Colin Firth for "The King's Speech." Lead actress in a drama is a bit harder to pin down, but it will probably be among Nicole Kidman for "Rabbit Hole," Jennifer Lawrence for "Winter's Bone" and Natalie Portman for "Black Swan."

And it's Depp versus Depp for lead actor in a comedy or musical feature film. Depp is competing against himself with nominations for "The Tourist" as well as his eclectic turn as the Mad Hatter in "Alice in Wonderland."

The stars of "The Kids Are All Right" -- Annette Bening and Julianne Moore -- are the front-runners for lead actress in a comedy or musical, with Bening perceived as having an edge over Moore. Joining Bening, Moore and Jolie in the category are Oscar co-host Anne Hathaway for "Love & Other Drugs" and Emma Stone -- the dark horse -- for "Easy A."

The Globes are often considered an indicator as to what will win an Academy Award. But the HFPA and the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences don't always agree. Last year, the Globes awarded the dramatic film to "Avatar" with James Cameron winning for directing, while the academy gave best picture to "The Hurt Locker" and director to Kathryn Bigelow.

Robert De Niro will receive the Cecil B. DeMille Award.
